\chapter*{Abstract}\thispagestyle{rmplain}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
\label{05_abstract}
This thesis presents a proof-of-concept workflow for evaluating and improving building footprints in five study areas in the Global South using Google Open Buildings as the base dataset. In many regions, building footprints are essential for mapping, humanitarian response, planning, and geospatial analysis, yet authoritative reference data are often unavailable and automatically generated datasets may still contain positional, completeness, and geometric errors. Rather than generating a new building dataset from scratch, this work investigates whether a freely available global dataset can be locally assessed and selectively improved.

The proposed workflow combines a multimodal large language model (MLLM) with SAM-based segmentation in a two-step framework. The MLLM is used for quality assessment, workflow routing, and prompt-point generation, while the segmentation component generates refined building-mask candidates for polygons identified as problematic. Since SAM outputs raster-based segmentation masks, an additional post-processing step was required to convert and regularize the generated geometries into GIS-usable vector polygons. For this purpose, an existing building-regularization package was adapted to better preserve the irregular and incrementally grown building shapes observed in the study areas.

The workflow was evaluated through manual before-and-after classification of 1,256 building polygons. Within this manually evaluated subset, 82.8\% of the polygons improved after post-processing, while the share of polygons rated as \textit{good} or \textit{perfect} increased to 77.8\%. The strongest results were achieved in Niger and Mozambique, where 85.9\% and 83.8\% of evaluated polygons reached the \textit{good} or \textit{perfect} categories after processing. Liberia reached 79.5\%, while Mexico and Nepal reached 72.4\% and 65.6\%, respectively. These image-relative results indicate that the workflow performed best for simpler and more compact building geometries, while performance was lower in areas with more complex, heterogeneous, or incremental building structures. They describe geometric correctness with respect to the evaluated OpenAerialMap imagery, not necessarily absolute real-world footprint accuracy.

The MLLM was useful for coarse routing decisions and for generating approximate spatial prompt points, but it was not reliable enough for fine-grained semantic error descriptions or for judging whether a refined polygon was better than the original. SAM-based segmentation generally produced useful building-mask candidates, but the final quality depended strongly on careful polygonization and post-processing. The findings suggest that foundation models can support local building-footprint refinement, but they do not yet provide a fully automatic and reliable geospatial quality-assessment workflow. In its current form, the approach is best understood as a candidate-generation workflow that could support human-in-the-loop corrections, generate improvement candidates, and support local dataset maintenance rather than replace expert evaluation entirely.
