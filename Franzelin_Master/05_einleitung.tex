\chapter{Introduction}
\label{Introduction}

Building footprints are a fundamental component of geospatial information systems, supporting applications such as urban planning, infrastructure monitoring, population estimation, humanitarian response, and disaster management \citep{Awrangjeb_AutomaticExtraction, ZHENG_Buildingdamage, sirko2021continentalscalebuildingdetectionhigh}. Accurate and up-to-date footprint data are also essential for national mapping agencies, where building layers form part of continuously maintained topographic databases. However, the manual digitisation and validation of building footprints over large areas requires substantial time and expert labour, which has motivated the development of automated and semi-automated extraction methods \citep{rastogi2022automatic, Luo_overviewbuioldingfootprint}.

In recent years, advances in machine learning, high-resolution satellite imagery, and large-scale geospatial data processing have enabled the production of extensive open building-footprint datasets. A prominent example is the Google Open Buildings dataset, which was generated using deep-learning-based building detection from high-resolution satellite imagery and provides large-scale footprint coverage in regions where authoritative building data are often incomplete or unavailable \citep{sirko2021continentalscalebuildingdetectionhigh, googleopenbuildings}. Such datasets can provide valuable baseline information, particularly in data-scarce regions, but their quality is not spatially uniform. Building-footprint products may differ substantially in terms of completeness, footprint count, total building area, and local reliability, meaning that they cannot always be used without further quality assessment \citep{CHAMBERLAIN2024102104, herfort2023spatio}.

The quality assessment of building footprints is therefore a central challenge in GIScience. Existing approaches commonly evaluate positional accuracy, completeness, and geometric similarity by comparing building footprints against authoritative or manually validated reference data \citep{Hongchao_QualityAssesmentOSM, Brovelli_OSM_SpatialAccuracy}. While such reference-based methods are highly valuable, they are difficult to apply in regions where reliable reference datasets are missing, outdated, or inconsistent. At the same time, automated building-footprint extraction remains affected by recurring errors such as missing buildings, false detections, shifted polygons, incomplete outlines, merged buildings, and irregular geometries. These errors are partly caused by the diversity of building materials, roof shapes, settlement patterns, vegetation cover, shadows, and image conditions across different geographic regions \citep{sirko2021continentalscalebuildingdetectionhigh, shankar2017classificationrepresentationassessinggeodiversity, yin2023givlimprovinggeographicalinclusivity}.

This creates a need for quality-assessment workflows that can evaluate existing vector-based building footprints directly from imagery and support selective correction where necessary. Recent developments in foundation models offer new possibilities in this context \citep{awais2023foundationalmodelsdefiningnew}. Vision transformers and related architectures have shown that transformer-based models can capture global image context for visual recognition and dense prediction tasks \citep{DosovitskiyTransformer, ranftl2021visiontransformersdenseprediction}. More recently, visual foundation models such as the Segment Anything Model (SAM) have introduced promptable segmentation, enabling the generation of object masks from spatial prompts such as points or bounding boxes \citep{kirillov2023segment}. SAM 3 extends this model family with concept-based segmentation, which can use text prompts to identify object categories \citep{papersam3}. In parallel, multimodal large language models (MLLMs) combine visual and textual information and can perform image-based reasoning, description, and evaluation tasks \citep{wu2023multimodallargelanguagemodels, Yin_2024MLLM}. These developments are increasingly relevant for GeoAI, although their transfer to geospatial quality assessment and vector-data update workflows remains limited \citep{mai2023opportunitieschallengesfoundationmodels, Ji_2025_foundationGeospatiolreasoning}.

This thesis investigates whether such models can be combined for the visual quality assessment and selective refinement of existing building footprints. Rather than generating a complete building dataset from scratch, the proposed workflow follows an update-oriented perspective \citep{Qin_changedetection, Wadembere_GeometryAdjustment}: existing polygons are first assessed and only selected features are passed to a refinement stage. This distinction is important because many practical geospatial production environments already contain building datasets that require validation, correction, or incremental updating rather than full reconstruction \cite{Wadembere_GeometryAdjustment}.

The proposed workflow is structured into two main stages. In the first stage, building polygons from Google Open Buildings are evaluated by a multimodal large language model using aerial image patches. The MLLM assesses whether the existing polygon corresponds to a visible building, identifies potential quality problems, and generates spatial prompts where refinement is required. In the second stage, these prompts are used to guide SAM-based segmentation. The resulting masks are converted into vector geometries and regularized into GIS-ready building-footprint candidates.

The motivation for this two-step design is that assessment and correction require different model capabilities. The MLLM is used for contextual visual reasoning and decision-making, while SAM is used for precise mask generation. By decoupling these tasks, the workflow allows more transparent control over when a footprint should be refined, which type of prompt should be used, and how the resulting geometry should be post-processed. This separation also makes the workflow more flexible than a purely end-to-end extraction approach, as it can be applied to existing building datasets and adapted to different geographic settings without requiring a complete retraining process.

The research gap addressed in this thesis lies in the limited application of multimodal, feedback-driven foundation-model workflows to vector-based geospatial quality assessment. Deep-learning methods for building extraction and foundation models for visual segmentation have been widely studied \citep{rastogi2022automatic, Luo_overviewbuioldingfootprint, sirko2021continentalscalebuildingdetectionhigh, kirillov2023segment}. However, less attention has been given to workflows that combine MLLM-based visual assessment, prompt generation, segmentation, and geometric post-processing for the selective refinement of existing building footprints \citep{wu2023multimodallargelanguagemodels, Yin_2024MLLM, mai2023opportunitieschallengesfoundationmodels, Ji_2025_foundationGeospatiolreasoning, Hongchao_QualityAssesmentOSM, Brovelli_OSM_SpatialAccuracy}.

Based on this gap, the aim of this thesis is to evaluate whether multimodal and visual foundation models can support image-relative quality assessment and selective refinement of existing building-footprint polygons in selected study areas of the Global South. The thesis addresses the following research questions:

\textbf{RQ1:} To what extent can a multimodal large language model support the visual quality assessment of existing vector-based building footprints?

\textbf{RQ2:} How effectively can MLLM-generated spatial prompts, combined with SAM-based segmentation and geometric post-processing, improve selected building-footprint geometries?

\textbf{RQ3:} How transferable is the proposed workflow across different geographic regions, building morphologies, and imagery conditions?

In this thesis, the term \textit{building footprint} is used because it is the established terminology in remote-sensing and geospatial literature for vector polygons representing buildings derived from aerial or satellite imagery \citep{sirko2021continentalscalebuildingdetectionhigh, rastogi2022automatic}. However, it is important to note that the visual evidence available to the MLLM and SAM is based on overhead imagery and therefore primarily represents the visible roof or building outline rather than the true ground-level footprint. Accordingly, the term building footprint is retained for consistency with existing datasets and literature, while the assessment and refinement performed in this study should be understood as targeting image-visible building or roof outlines.
