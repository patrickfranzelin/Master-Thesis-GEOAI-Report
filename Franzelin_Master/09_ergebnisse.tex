\chapter{Results}
\label{08_ergebnisse}

As mentioned in Section~\ref{ch:study_area}, the workflow was applied to five different study areas in the Global South. In this chapter, the resulting building detections and final workflow outputs are presented and evaluated. The focus is on how the proposed workflow changes the original Google Open Buildings polygons, how often the improvement step leads to better results, and in which situations the method still produces problematic outputs.

\section{Processed Dataset and Pipeline Coverage}

\subsection{MLQA Classification and Manual Evaluation Sample}

In total, the workflow was applied to 2,842 building polygons from the Google Open Buildings dataset. The mutually exclusive MLQA routing classes comprised 1,283 cases classified as \textit{No visible}, 971 as \textit{Partial house}, and 588 as \textit{Full house}. Thus, 1,559 buildings were routed as visible partial- or full-house cases. A SAM-derived polygon was available for 1,518 buildings and a final workflow output for 1,352 buildings.

From these outputs, 1,256 buildings were manually evaluated, as described in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_strategy}. The number of evaluated buildings is not the same for all countries. This is mainly because some Google Open Buildings detections did not have a clearly corresponding visible building in the selected imagery and were therefore removed from the dataset. In addition, the evaluation was only possible when the original Google Open Buildings polygon, the SAM-derived polygon, and the final workflow output could clearly be matched to each other. In some cases, not all three versions could be assigned reliably, so these buildings were excluded from the manual evaluation.

The denominators therefore differ between analyses. The initial MLQA input always refers to all 2,842 Google Open Buildings polygons. The manual before-and-after evaluation refers to the 1,256 cases for which original, SAM-derived, and final workflow geometries could be compared. Some geometry-specific analyses use \(n=1,253\) because three evaluated cases could not be included in that specific calculation.

In this step, the Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct model was used to analyze each building detection before the subsequent refinement stages were applied. The model first identified whether a building was clearly visible in the selected imagery. If the building was visible, the model then generated positive and negative prompt points for the SAM-based segmentation and additionally produced a short qualitative description of the detected errors.

Buildings classified as \textit{No visible} were generally removed from further routing because no corresponding visible building could be identified reliably in the selected imagery; three such cases nevertheless remained in the evaluated subset. The distinction between \textit{Partial house} and \textit{Full house} is described in Section~\ref{sec:decision_logic_routing}. In this step, the model evaluated whether large parts of the visible building were cut off by the original Google Open Buildings polygon. Based on this classification, the workflow was routed differently for partial-house and full-house cases. The column \textit{Total evaluated} represents the subset of buildings for which final workflow outputs were available and manually evaluated.
Based on this manually evaluated subset, the following sections compare the original Google Open Buildings polygons, the SAM-derived intermediate geometries where relevant, and the final workflow outputs.

In the following sections, the term \textit{final workflow output} refers to the building polygon produced after the complete refinement workflow, including SAM-based segmentation, polygonization, geometric regularization, and database storage.

\section{Overall Quality Improvement}

\subsection{Transition from Original Quality to Final Workflow Output Quality}

The manual ratings indicate an overall improvement across all selected study areas. The original Google Open Buildings polygons often contained substantial geometric errors, irregular boundaries, or incomplete building outlines. None of the manually checked buildings was classified as \textit{perfect} in the original Google Open Buildings dataset before workflow processing.

Figure~\ref{fig:transition_heatmap} shows the transition from the original Google Open Buildings polygon quality to the quality of the final workflow output. The rows represent the manual rating of the original Google Open Buildings polygons, while the columns show the final manual rating after workflow processing. The majority of buildings that originally received a \textit{bad} or \textit{ok} rating received a higher final manual rating, most often \textit{good} or \textit{perfect}.

Out of the 581 buildings that were originally classified as \textit{bad}, 292 received a final rating of \textit{perfect} in the final workflow output. Another 153 buildings received a final rating of \textit{good}, while only 67 remained in the \textit{bad} category. Similarly, from the 656 buildings that originally received an \textit{ok} rating, 382 received a final rating of \textit{perfect} and 134 of \textit{good}. Only 49 buildings from this category were finally still classified as \textit{bad}.

The results also show that almost no buildings were initially classified as \textit{good}. Only 19 buildings started in this category, from which 10 improved further to \textit{perfect}. No building in the manually evaluated subset was already classified as \textit{perfect} before the workflow was applied. Among the final workflow outputs, the distribution comprised 116 \textit{bad}, 163 \textit{ok}, 293 \textit{good}, and 684 \textit{perfect} ratings.

\subsection{Improvement Rates by Study Area}

The complete refinement workflow improved the majority of evaluated building polygons in all study areas. Across the manually evaluated subset, 1,040 buildings (\textbf{82.8\%}) showed an improvement after workflow processing, while 164 (\textbf{13.1\%}) remained unchanged and 52 (\textbf{4.1\%}) were degraded.

The strongest overall improvements were observed in Niger and Mozambique. In Niger, \textbf{86.7\%} of the evaluated buildings improved, corresponding to 222 out of 256 manually checked samples; 9.0\% remained unchanged and 4.3\% degraded. Mozambique showed a very similar pattern, with \textbf{86.2\%} improved buildings (144 out of 167 samples), 10.8\% unchanged, and 3.0\% degraded. Liberia also produced strong results, where \textbf{86.1\%} of the evaluated polygons improved after workflow processing (327 out of 380 buildings), 12.6\% remained unchanged, and 1.3\% degraded. Lower, but still positive improvement rates were observed in Mexico and Nepal. In Mexico, \textbf{77.0\%} of the evaluated buildings improved (201 out of 261), while \textbf{17.2\%} remained unchanged and \textbf{5.7\%} degraded after workflow processing. Nepal showed the lowest overall improvement rate with \textbf{76.0\%} improved buildings (146 out of 192 samples), 15.6\% unchanged, and \textbf{8.3\%} degraded.

\section{Changes in Geometric Error Patterns}

During the manual evaluation, geometry error categories were additionally assigned to each building polygon. The complete taxonomy is described in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_criteria_error_taxonomy}. Five principal error categories were defined: \texttt{SHIFTED}, \texttt{SHAPE\_MISMATCH}, \texttt{MISSING\_PARTS}, \texttt{EXTRA\_PARTS}, and \texttt{OVERSIMPLIFIED}. Multiple categories could be assigned to the same polygon, so the percentages reported for these categories are not mutually exclusive.

\subsection{Error-category Shifts Before and After Workflow Processing}

Figure~\ref{fig:error_profile_by_location} summarizes the distribution of error categories before and after workflow processing for each study area and for the complete manually evaluated dataset.

\begin{figure}[htb]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Franzelin_Master/img/02_error_categories_by_location_total.png}
	\caption{Distribution of multi-label geometry error categories before and after workflow processing for each study area and for the complete manually evaluated dataset (\(n=1,256\)).}
	\label{fig:error_profile_by_location}
\end{figure}

The dominant error type in the original Google Open Buildings polygons was \texttt{SHIFTED}. Across the complete manually evaluated subset, 971 buildings (\textbf{77.3\%}) exhibited a noticeable spatial displacement between the polygon geometry and the visible roof structure in the aerial imagery. The highest prevalence of this error category was observed in Mozambique (\textbf{93.4\%}), followed by Niger (\textbf{81.6\%}) and Liberia (\textbf{77.6\%}).

The complete refinement workflow reduced the occurrence of shifted polygons in all study areas. Across the complete dataset, the proportion of buildings classified as shifted decreased from 971 (\textbf{77.3\%}) to 54 (\textbf{4.3\%}). The largest reduction was observed in Mozambique, where the share of shifted polygons decreased from \textbf{93.4\%} to \textbf{3.6\%}. Comparable reductions were observed in Liberia (\textbf{77.6\%} to \textbf{5.8\%}) and Niger (\textbf{81.6\%} to \textbf{5.1\%}).

The second most frequent error category in the original Google Open Buildings dataset was \texttt{SHAPE\_MISMATCH}, which decreased from 474 buildings (\textbf{37.7\%}) to 164 (\textbf{13.1\%}). This category describes cases in which the polygon geometry did not adequately follow the visible building outline. Nepal showed the highest proportion of shape mismatch errors, affecting \textbf{61.5\%} of the evaluated buildings. Although the complete refinement workflow reduced this error category in all study areas, it remained comparatively frequent in Nepal after workflow processing (\textbf{25.0\%}).

In contrast, the relative occurrence of the categories \texttt{MISSING\_PARTS} and \texttt{EXTRA\_PARTS} increased in the final workflow outputs. Across the full manually evaluated dataset, missing parts increased from 95 buildings (\textbf{7.6\%}) to 198 (\textbf{15.8\%}), while extra parts increased from 31 (\textbf{2.5\%}) to 134 (\textbf{10.7\%}). The possible reasons for these increases, including the role of SAM-derived intermediate geometries and geometric post-processing, are examined in Section~\ref{09_diskussion}. In such cases, roof sections were either omitted or additional geometry was introduced beyond the visible building outline.

The category \texttt{OVERSIMPLIFIED} was comparatively rare both before and after workflow processing. Across the complete dataset, its frequency decreased from 171 buildings (\textbf{13.6\%}) in the original Google Open Buildings dataset to 27 (\textbf{2.1\%}) in the final workflow outputs.

Polygons classified as \textit{perfect} in the final workflow output were not assigned any remaining geometry error category and therefore do not contribute to the error distributions after workflow processing shown in Figure~\ref{fig:error_profile_by_location}.

\subsection{New Errors in the Final Workflow Output and Tree Context}
The manual evaluation also recorded whether the final workflow output contained a new geometric error that was not present in the SAM-based segmentation. Across the complete manually evaluated dataset, this occurred in 207 of 1,256 buildings (\textbf{16.5\%}).

Since vegetation was one of the visible difficulties in several study areas, the relation between detected tree context and new errors in the final workflow output was checked at building level. This analysis does not measure confirmed tree occlusions, because the database only stores detected tree masks and not whether a tree dent was actually detected and repaired on the building boundary. It should therefore be interpreted only as a proxy for vegetation-influenced image context. Cases with no tree mask were classified as \textit{no detected tree}; tree-area ratios up to 0.25 as \textit{low}, ratios above 0.25 and up to 1.0 as \textit{medium}, and ratios above 1.0 as \textit{high tree context}.

Buildings with detected trees showed a higher share of new errors in the final workflow output than buildings without detected trees. The aggregated detected-tree group corresponds to the combined low, medium, and high classes. The new-error rate increased from 13.4\% without detected trees to 18.7\% among the 726 buildings in the aggregated detected-tree group. In high tree-context cases, the rate reached 23.0\%. The binary association was weak (\(\phi=0.07\)), so tree context alone does not explain the failures, but the descriptive pattern is consistent with higher difficulty in vegetation-rich image contexts.

\section{Spatial Shift Analysis}

\subsection{Magnitude and Direction of Positional Corrections}

Since \texttt{SHIFTED} was the dominant error category in the original Google Open Buildings dataset, the spatial displacement of these 971 cases was analyzed in more detail. For this purpose, centroid shift vectors were calculated between the original Google Open Buildings polygons labeled as \texttt{SHIFTED} and the corresponding final workflow geometries using PostGIS geography distances in meters. The aim of this analysis was to assess whether the displacement followed a homogeneous direction that could have been corrected by a simple global translation before running the workflow.


\begin{figure}[htb]
	\centering  
	\includegraphics[scale=0.65]{Franzelin_Master/img/04_post_shift_vector_scatter_by_country.png}
	\caption{Centroid displacement vectors for originally shifted buildings by study area (\(n=971\)).}
	\label{fig:error_profile_shift}
\end{figure}

The scatterplots in Figure~\ref{fig:error_profile_shift} show that centroid displacement patterns differ substantially between study areas. Liberia, Mexico, and Nepal exhibit broadly distributed shift vectors, with considerable variation in both displacement magnitude and direction. Nepal shows the largest spread (\(n=124\), \(sd_{dx}=2.61\,\mathrm{m}\), \(sd_{dy}=3.80\,\mathrm{m}\), \(p90=8.88\,\mathrm{m}\)), followed by Mexico (\(n=187\), \(sd_{dx}=1.95\,\mathrm{m}\), \(sd_{dy}=2.94\,\mathrm{m}\), \(p90=7.20\,\mathrm{m}\)). Liberia exhibits a lower but still noticeable variability (\(n=295\), \(sd_{dx}=1.29\,\mathrm{m}\), \(sd_{dy}=1.61\,\mathrm{m}\), \(p90=4.68\,\mathrm{m}\)).

Niger and Mozambique show more concentrated displacement patterns with lower spread values (Niger: \(n=209\), \(sd_{dx}=0.85\,\mathrm{m}\), \(sd_{dy}=0.78\,\mathrm{m}\); Mozambique: \(n=156\), \(sd_{dx}=0.79\,\mathrm{m}\), \(sd_{dy}=0.86\,\mathrm{m}\)). However, despite this clustering, displacement directions and magnitudes still vary within both countries.

\subsection{Area Preservation}

Figure~\ref{fig:area_comparison} compares original Google Open Buildings areas with final workflow output building areas across all study areas. The signed area change was calculated as \(100(A_{final}/A_{original}-1)\), and the analysis included the 1,253 buildings with available final workflow geometry. For readability, values above the 98th percentile of either axis are omitted from the scatterplot, while the reported percentages use all 1,253 buildings. The scatterplot shows a positive relationship between original Google Open Buildings area and final workflow output building area in all countries. However, the spread around the 1:1 reference line differs between study areas.

Niger and Mozambique show the strongest agreement with the identity line and the lowest variability. Niger exhibits comparatively stable area preservation, with 39.1\% remaining within \(\pm10\%\). In Mozambique, 52.7\% of buildings remain within \(\pm10\%\) of their original Google Open Buildings area, while only 2.4\% become more than 50\% larger in the final workflow output.

Liberia shows an intermediate pattern, with 32.1\% remaining within \(\pm10\%\), but also a considerable proportion of strong positive deviations (20.2\%). In contrast, Mexico and Nepal show broader dispersion patterns. Only 18.8\% of buildings in Mexico and 13.0\% in Nepal remain within \(\pm10\%\) of the original Google Open Buildings area. Furthermore, strong positive deviations occur more frequently, with 31.8\% of Mexican and 27.1\% of Nepalese buildings increasing by more than 50\% relative to their original Google Open Buildings footprint area.
\subsection{Influence of Google Open Buildings Confidence}

The Google Open Buildings dataset includes a confidence value for each detection. This confidence was compared with the manual quality score of the original Google Open Buildings polygons for all 1,256 evaluated buildings. Pearson's correlation was \(r=0.32\), while Spearman's rank correlation was \(\rho=0.31\). This indicates that higher Google confidence values tended to be associated with better original Google Open Buildings polygon quality, although the relationship was only moderate.

\subsection{Original, SAM-derived, and Final Workflow Geometry Complexity}

The intermediate SAM-derived geometry was also analyzed separately from the final workflow geometry. This shows the roles of SAM-based segmentation and geometric post-processing more clearly. Vertex counts were calculated with \texttt{ST\_NPoints} on valid geometries and include all rings, multipart components, and ring-closing coordinates. The SAM-derived output had a much higher average vertex count than both the original Google Open Buildings polygons and the final workflow outputs. On average, the original Google Open Buildings polygons had 5.27 vertices, the SAM-derived polygons had 40.81 vertices, and the final workflow outputs had 13.45 vertices. The three final workflow geometries unavailable for this calculation were Liberia cases without a stored regularized geometry, resulting in \(n=1,253\) for the final stage.

\subsection{\textit{Full house} and \textit{Partial house} Cases}
\label{sec:results_full_partial_cases}

The comparison between \textit{Full house} and \textit{Partial house} cases included the 1,253 manually evaluated buildings assigned to one of the two visible-building routing branches. Buildings classified as \textit{Full house} (\(n=530\)) reached \textit{good} or \textit{perfect} final ratings in 79.6\% of cases, with a median absolute area change of 13.5\% relative to the original Google Open Buildings footprint. Buildings classified as \textit{Partial house} (\(n=723\)) reached \textit{good} or \textit{perfect} final ratings in 76.3\% of cases. The contrast value reports the median absolute linear Weber contrast between the building and its local surrounding ring after sRGB linearization. The texture value reports the median Euclidean distance between the building and surrounding texture descriptors. The strongest brightness separation was observed in Niger, followed by Mexico, Liberia, Nepal, and Mozambique. For example, Liberia showed only moderate brightness contrast but the highest texture difference, which is consistent with buildings being visually distinguishable from vegetated surroundings through texture and surface structure rather than brightness alone. Overall, the results suggest that the visual conditions for building extraction differed substantially between the study areas and that brightness contrast and texture contrast captured complementary aspects of building visibility.

The results show that both datasets achieved high precision and moderate to high recall against the TLM reference subset. At the strictest IoU threshold of 0.50, the workflow output reached a precision of \textbf{0.800}, a recall of \textbf{0.697} and an F1-score of \textbf{0.745}. The Microsoft source polygons performed slightly better at the same threshold, with a precision of \textbf{0.812}, a recall of \textbf{0.725} and an F1-score of \textbf{0.766}.

This indicates that the regularization step did not improve the footprint agreement with the authoritative Swiss reference data in this test. Instead, it slightly reduced recall and F1-score compared to the Microsoft source polygons.

\section{MLLM Outputs: Semantic Descriptions and Spatial Prompts}
\label{sec:results_mllm_outputs}

The MLLM produced two output types that were evaluated separately: semantic descriptions of the discrepancy between the original Google Open Buildings polygon and the visible building, and spatial point prompts used to guide SAM. The following subsections first compare the semantic outputs with the manual error labels and then evaluate whether the generated positive and negative points were spatially consistent with successful final polygons.

During the development of the workflow, it became apparent that generating consistent and reliable semantic descriptions with the 8B model was challenging. Therefore, two different semantic-description approaches were investigated.

The first approach provided the MLLM with predefined semantic error categories and allowed it to select the categories that best matched the observed geometric discrepancy. The second approach used an unrestricted prompt, allowing the model to generate short human-readable error descriptions without predefined categories.

\subsection{Predefined Error Categories}
\label{sec:results_predefined_error_categories}
For the predefined category approach, 1,256 polygon pairs were manually evaluated and compared against the MLLM-generated labels. The manually assigned labels originated from the original Google building polygons and served as reference data.

Overall, only 53 cases (4.2\%) showed an exact category-set agreement between manual interpretation and MLLM prediction. The mean Jaccard similarity between category sets reached 0.408, with a median of 0.333.

Another difference was the number of assigned semantic categories per building. Manual interpretation assigned an average of 1.39 categories per building, whereas the MLQA workflow assigned 2.64 categories per building. 

The semantic categories used in the workflow were:

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{MISALIGNED}/\texttt{SHIFTED}: polygon position mismatch determined through edge comparison.
\item \texttt{SHAPE\_MISMATCH}: incorrect building footprint geometry excluding pure positional displacement.
\item \texttt{MISSING\_PARTS}: roof segments visible in the imagery are not covered by the polygon.
\item \texttt{EXTRA\_PARTS}: the polygon includes surrounding non-building areas.

\end{itemize}


The model output \texttt{MISALIGNED} was normalized to the manual category \texttt{SHIFTED}. The structured prompt did not request \texttt{OVERSIMPLIFIED}, which consequently had no structured MLQA predictions in this comparison. Among the requested semantic categories, \texttt{SHIFTED} was the most frequently assigned category by the MLQA workflow. Since positional displacement was evaluated through a dedicated prompt, the category was assigned in 97.6\% of all cases, compared to 77.3\% in the manual interpretation.

The largest discrepancy was observed for \texttt{EXTRA\_PARTS}. The MLQA workflow assigned this category in 72.6\% of all cases, whereas manual interpretation assigned it in only 2.5\% of buildings.

\subsection{Free-text Error Descriptions}
\label{sec:results_free_text_descriptions}

In addition to the predefined semantic category approach, unrestricted free-text descriptions generated by the MLLM were evaluated separately. In this setup, the model generated short human-readable explanations describing the mismatch between the original Google Open Buildings polygon and the visible building structure in the aerial image. An example generated description is:

\textit{"The green polygon has missing parts and extra parts compared to the building outline, with some sections of the building not enclosed by the polygon and the polygon extending beyond the building's actual footprint."}

As described in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_semantic_error_descriptions}, the generated descriptions were mapped back into the predefined semantic taxonomy using a rule-based keyword extraction procedure.

After filtering placeholder outputs and parsing failures, 1,250 building cases remained available for evaluation. Across the complete dataset, only 16 cases (\textbf{1.3\%}) showed an exact category-set agreement between manual interpretation and semantic categories extracted from the generated free-text descriptions. The mean Jaccard similarity reached \textbf{0.22}, with a median similarity of \textbf{0.25}.

The extracted semantic descriptions also assigned more categories per building than the manual evaluation. Manual interpretation assigned an average of \textbf{1.39} categories per building, whereas the free-text extraction assigned \textbf{2.38} categories on average.

The strongest performance was observed for the category \texttt{SHIFTED}, which reached a precision of \textbf{0.801}, a recall of \textbf{0.509}, and an F1-score of \textbf{0.623}. \texttt{SHAPE\_MISMATCH} reached an F1-score of \textbf{0.426}.

Strong overprediction behavior was visible for \texttt{EXTRA\_PARTS} and \texttt{MISSING\_PARTS}. The extraction procedure assigned \texttt{EXTRA\_PARTS} 956 times although manual interpretation identified only 31 occurrences. Similarly, \texttt{MISSING\_PARTS} was extracted 898 times compared to 95 manually assigned cases.

In addition to the rule-based keyword analysis, a smaller qualitative evaluation was performed for the generated free-text descriptions in a separate evaluation interface. The evaluation was stopped after this subset because the generated descriptions showed strong repetition. Many sentences followed nearly the same structure and repeatedly described the polygon as having \textit{missing parts}, \textit{extra parts}, or both, even when the specific visual error differed.

After keeping the latest evaluation for each building, 99 unique sentence evaluations remained. Of these, only 11 sentences were classified as \textit{correct}. A further 25 were classified as \textit{partly correct}. The largest group was \textit{too vague}, with 37 cases, while 26 sentences were classified as \textit{wrong}. Thus, 36 of 99 descriptions were at least partly correct.

\subsection{Spatial Accuracy of MLLM Point Prompts}
\label{sec:results_mllm_point_prompts}

The point-in-polygon analysis was possible for 320 manually rated \textit{perfect} buildings in the \textit{Full house} routing class. Across these cases, 885 of 960 positive points were located inside or on the boundary of the corresponding polygon, resulting in a point-level agreement of 92.19\%. At the stricter building level, all three positive points were correctly placed for 256 buildings (80.00\%). Agreement was 91.56\% for the first positive point, 96.56\% for the second, and 88.44\% for the third. Most errors were small boundary-related deviations: 51 of the 75 missed positive points were located within 0.5\,m of the polygon boundary. The negative-point analysis contained 963 points because one additional Mexico case retained a three-point negative prompt set but no corresponding positive prompt set. Of these, 881 were correctly located outside the building polygons (91.48\%).
