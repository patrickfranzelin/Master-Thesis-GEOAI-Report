\chapter{Results}
\label{08_ergebnisse}

As mentioned in Section~\ref{ch:study_area}, the workflow was applied to five different study areas in the Global South. In this chapter, the resulting building detections and post-processing outputs are presented and evaluated. The focus is on how the proposed workflow changes the original building polygons, how often the improvement step leads to better results, and in which situations the method still produces problematic outputs.

\section{Processed Dataset and Pipeline Coverage}

\subsection{MLQA Classification and Manual Evaluation Sample}

In total, the code was run on 2’842 houses from the Google Open Buildings dataset. These buildings were processed, stored and rectangularized. From these, 1’633 houses were additionally processed with the complete improvement workflow. Since this step required relatively long processing times, the number of buildings was mostly limited to around 600 houses per run and per study area. The remaining 1’577 buildings were therefore only processed with the direct global regularisation algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:rectangularization}). During the workflow, a total of 1’283 buildings were identified as incorrect or problematic detections within the Google Open Buildings dataset.

From the 1’633 houses that passed through the complete workflow, 1’256 buildings were manually evaluated, as described in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_strategy}. The number of evaluated buildings is not the same for all countries. This is mainly because some buildings from the Google Open Buildings dataset were identified as false positives and were therefore removed from the dataset. In addition, the evaluation was only possible when the original detection, the SAM result and the final post-processed polygon could clearly be matched to each other. In some cases, not all three versions could be assigned reliably, so these buildings were excluded from the manual evaluation.

MLQA stands for \textit{Machine Learning Quality Assurance}. In this step, the Qwen3 8B model was used to analyse each building detection before the actual post-processing workflow was applied. The model first identified whether a building was clearly visible in the satellite image. If the building was visible, the model then generated positive and negative prompt points for the SAM segmentation and additionally produced a short qualitative description of the detected errors.

Buildings classified as \textit{No visible} were automatically removed from further processing because the underlying building could not be identified reliably in the imagery. The distinction between \textit{Partial house} and \textit{Full house} is described in Section~\ref{sec:decision_logic_routing}. In this step, the model evaluated whether large parts of the visible building were cut off by the original polygon. Based on this classification, the workflow was routed differently for partially covered and fully covered houses. The column \textit{Total evaluated} represents the subset of buildings that were finally post-processed and manually evaluated.
Based on this manually evaluated subset, the following sections compare the original detections, the directly regularised polygons and the improved post-processing results.

\section{Overall Quality Improvement}

\subsection{Transition from Original to Post-processed Quality}

In general, the improvement produced by the workflow was clearly visible across all selected study areas. The original polygons often contained substantial geometric errors, irregular boundaries or incomplete building outlines. None of the manually checked buildings was classified as \textit{perfect} in the original dataset before post-processing.

Figure~\ref{fig:transition_heatmap} shows the transition from the original polygon quality to the post-processed quality after applying the workflow. The rows represent the original rating of the building polygons, while the columns show the final rating after post-processing. The majority of buildings that originally received a \textit{bad} or \textit{ok} rating were improved towards the categories \textit{good} and especially \textit{perfect}. 

Out of the 584 buildings that were originally classified as \textit{bad}, 293 were transformed into \textit{perfect} polygons after post-processing. Another 155 buildings improved to the category \textit{good}, while only 67 remained in the \textit{bad} category. Similarly, from the 659 buildings that originally received an \textit{ok} rating, 384 were improved to \textit{perfect} and 134 to \textit{good}. Only 49 buildings from this category were finally still classified as \textit{bad}. 

The results also show that almost no buildings were initially classified as \textit{good}. Only 20 buildings started in this category, from which 10 improved further to \textit{perfect}. No building in the manually evaluated subset was already classified as \textit{perfect} before the workflow was applied.

\subsection{Improvement Rates by Study Area}

The post-processing workflow improved the majority of evaluated building polygons in all study areas. Averaged across all five locations, \textbf{82.3\%} of the manually evaluated buildings showed an improvement after post-processing, while \textbf{12.9\%} remained largely unchanged and \textbf{4.6\%} were degraded. 

The strongest overall improvements were observed in Niger and Mozambique. In Niger, \textbf{86.7\%} of the evaluated buildings improved, corresponding to 222 out of 256 manually checked samples. Mozambique showed a very similar behaviour, with \textbf{86.2\%} improved buildings (144 out of 167 samples). Liberia also produced strong results, where \textbf{85.8\%} of the evaluated polygons improved after processing (332 out of 387 buildings). Lower, but still clearly positive improvement rates were observed in Mexico and Nepal. In Mexico, \textbf{77.0\%} of the evaluated buildings improved (201 out of 261), while \textbf{17.2\%} remained unchanged and \textbf{5.7\%} degraded slightly after processing. Nepal showed the lowest overall improvement rate with \textbf{76.0\%} improved buildings (146 out of 192 samples) and also the highest degradation share, where \textbf{8.3\%} of the polygons received a worse rating after post-processing.

\section{Changes in Error Composition}

\subsection{Dominant Errors in Original and Post-processed Geometries}

During the manual evaluation, geometry error categories were additionally assigned to each building polygon. The complete taxonomy is described in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_criteria_error_taxonomy}. Five principal error categories were defined: \textit{SHIFTED}, \textit{SHAPE\_MISMATCH}, \textit{OVERSIMPLIFIED}, \textit{MISSING\_PARTS}, and \textit{EXTRA\_PARTS}. The evaluation further recorded whether the post-processing workflow introduced a new geometric error that was not present in the original polygon. Across the complete manually evaluated dataset, this occurred in \textbf{16.6\%} of all evaluated buildings.

\subsection{Detected Tree Context and New Post-processing Errors}

Since vegetation was one of the visible difficulties in several study areas, the relation between detected tree context and newly introduced errors was also checked at building level. This analysis does not measure confirmed tree occlusions, because the database only stores detected tree masks and not whether a tree dent was actually detected and repaired on the building boundary. It should therefore be interpreted only as a proxy for vegetation-influenced image context. Still, buildings with detected trees showed a higher share of newly introduced post-processing errors than buildings without detected trees. The new-error rate increased from 13.4\% without detected trees to 18.7\% with detected trees. In high tree-context cases, the rate reached 23.0\%. The binary association was weak (\(\phi=0.07\)), so tree context alone does not explain the failures, but it indicates that vegetation-rich scenes are more difficult for the workflow.

Figure~\ref{fig:error_profile_by_location} summarizes the distribution of error categories before and after post-processing. The dominant error type in the original Google Open Buildings dataset was \textit{SHIFTED}. Across the complete manually evaluated subset, \textbf{77.4\%} of all buildings exhibited a noticeable spatial displacement between the polygon geometry and the visible roof structure in the aerial imagery. The highest prevalence of this error category was observed in Mozambique (\textbf{93.4\%}), followed by Niger (\textbf{81.6\%}) and Liberia (\textbf{78.0\%}).

The post-processing workflow reduced the occurrence of shifted polygons in all study areas. Across the complete dataset, the proportion of buildings classified as shifted decreased from \textbf{77.4\%} to \textbf{4.4\%}. The largest reduction was observed in Mozambique, where the share of shifted polygons decreased from \textbf{93.4\%} to \textbf{3.6\%}. Comparable reductions were observed in Liberia (\textbf{78.0\%} to \textbf{5.9\%}) and Niger (\textbf{81.6\%} to \textbf{5.1\%}).

The second most frequent error category in the original dataset was \textit{SHAPE\_MISMATCH}. This category describes cases in which the polygon geometry did not adequately follow the visible building outline. Nepal showed the highest proportion of shape mismatch errors, affecting \textbf{61.5\%} of the evaluated buildings. Although the post-processing workflow reduced this error category in all study areas, it remained comparatively frequent in Nepal after processing (\textbf{25.0\%}).

In contrast, the relative occurrence of the categories \textit{MISSING\_PARTS} and \textit{EXTRA\_PARTS} increased after post-processing. Across the full manually evaluated dataset, the proportion of polygons containing missing parts increased from \textbf{7.5\%} to \textbf{15.8\%}, while the proportion containing extra parts increased from \textbf{2.5\%} to \textbf{10.6\%}. These error types are primarily associated with segmentation artefacts introduced during the SAM-based reconstruction and with geometric simplifications applied during the polygon regularisation stage. In such cases, roof sections were either omitted or additional geometry was introduced beyond the actual building outline.

The category \textit{OVERSIMPLIFIED} was comparatively rare both before and after processing. Across the complete dataset, its frequency decreased from \textbf{13.6\%} in the original dataset to \textbf{2.1\%} after post-processing.

It should be noted that multiple error categories could be assigned simultaneously to a single building polygon. Consequently, the cumulative percentages within a study area may exceed 100\%. Furthermore, polygons classified as \textit{perfect} after post-processing were not assigned any remaining geometry error category and therefore do not contribute to the post-processing error distributions shown in Figure~\ref{fig:error_profile_by_location}.

\section{Spatial Shift Analysis}

\subsection{Magnitude and Direction of Positional Corrections}

Since \textit{SHIFTED} was the dominant error category in the original dataset, the spatial displacement of these cases was analysed in more detail. For this purpose, centroid shift vectors were calculated between the original polygons labelled as \textit{SHIFTED} and the corresponding post-processed polygons. The aim of this analysis was to assess whether the displacement followed a homogeneous direction that could have been corrected by a simple global translation before running the workflow.


\begin{figure}[htb]
	\centering  
	\includegraphics[scale=0.65]{Franzelin_Master/img/04_post_shift_vector_scatter_by_country.png}
	\caption{Centroid displacement vectors for originally shifted buildings by study area.}
	\label{fig:error_profile_shift}
\end{figure}

The scatterplots in Figure~\ref{fig:error_profile_shift} show that centroid displacement patterns differ substantially between study areas. Liberia, Mexico, and Nepal exhibit broadly distributed shift vectors, with considerable variation in both displacement magnitude and direction. Nepal shows the largest spread (\(sd_{dx}=2.61\,\mathrm{m}\), \(sd_{dy}=3.80\,\mathrm{m}\), \(p90=8.88\,\mathrm{m}\)), followed by Mexico (\(sd_{dx}=1.95\,\mathrm{m}\), \(sd_{dy}=2.94\,\mathrm{m}\), \(p90=7.20\,\mathrm{m}\)). Liberia exhibits a lower but still noticeable variability (\(sd_{dx}=1.29\,\mathrm{m}\), \(sd_{dy}=1.61\,\mathrm{m}\), \(p90=4.68\,\mathrm{m}\)).

Mozambique and Niger show more concentrated displacement patterns with lower spread values (Mozambique: \(sd_{dx}=0.79\,\mathrm{m}\), \(sd_{dy}=0.86\,\mathrm{m}\); Niger: \(sd_{dx}=0.85\,\mathrm{m}\), \(sd_{dy}=0.78\,\mathrm{m}\)). However, despite this clustering, displacement directions and magnitudes still vary within both countries.

The MLQA routing classes also showed different levels of difficulty. \section{Qualitative Semantic Error Description}

One component of the research question and workflow was to investigate whether a multimodal large language model (MLLM) can generate short, semantic, human-readable descriptions explaining the error cause between the original building polygon and the visible building structure in aerial imagery.

During the development of the workflow, it became apparent that generating constant and reliable semantic descriptions with the 8B model was challenging. Therefore, two different approaches were investigated.

The first approach provided the MLLM with predefined semantic error categories and allowed it to select the categories that best matched the observed geometric discrepancy. The advantage of this approach was that it produced a standardized output syntax, enabling direct statistical evaluation and comparison with manually assigned labels.

The second approach used an unrestricted prompt, allowing the model to generate short human-readable error descriptions without predefined categories. The motivation behind this approach was to obtain more interpretable semantic descriptions that resemble human explanations. However, these free-text outputs proved considerably more difficult to evaluate quantitatively due to varying wording and description detail.

\subsection{Predefined error category}
For the predefined category approach, 1256 polygon pairs were manually evaluated and compared against the MLLM-generated labels. The manually assigned labels originated from the original Google building polygons and served as reference data.

Overall, only 4.2\% of all cases showed an exact category-set agreement between manual interpretation and MLLM prediction. The mean Jaccard similarity between category sets reached 0.41.

Another difference was the number of assigned semantic categories per building. Manual interpretation assigned an average of 1.39 categories per building, whereas the MLQA workflow assigned 2.64 categories per building. 

The semantic categories used in the workflow were:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{MISALIGNED (SHIFTED)} Polygon position mismatch determined through edge comparison (top, bottom, left, right). If two or more sides are not aligned, the polygon is classified as shifted.
\item \textbf{SHAPE MISMATCH} Incorrect building footprint geometry excluding rotational errors.
\item \textbf{MISSING PARTS} Roof segments visible in the aerial image are not covered by the polygon.
\item \textbf{EXTRA PARTS} Polygon includes surrounding non-building areas.

\end{itemize}


Among the four semantic categories, \texttt{SHIFTED} was the most frequently assigned category by the MLQA workflow. Since positional displacement was evaluated through a dedicated prompt, the category was assigned in 97.6\% of all cases, compared to 77.3\% in the manual interpretation.

The largest discrepancy was observed for \texttt{EXTRA\_PARTS}. The MLQA workflow assigned this category in 72.6\% of all cases, whereas manual interpretation assigned it in only 2.5\% of buildings.

\subsection{Free-text Error Description}

In addition to the predefined semantic category approach, unrestricted free-text descriptions generated by the MLLM were evaluated separately. In this setup, the model generated short human-readable explanations describing the mismatch between the original polygon and the visible building structure in the aerial image. An example generated description is:

\textit{"The green polygon has missing parts and extra parts compared to the building outline, with some sections of the building not enclosed by the polygon and the polygon extending beyond the building's actual footprint."}

As described in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_semantic_error_descriptions}, the generated descriptions were mapped back into the predefined semantic taxonomy using a rule-based keyword extraction procedure.

After filtering placeholder outputs and parsing failures, approximately 1250 building cases remained available for evaluation. Across the complete dataset, only \textbf{1.3\%} of all buildings showed an exact category-set agreement between manual interpretation and semantic categories extracted from the generated free-text descriptions. The mean Jaccard similarity reached \textbf{0.22}, with a median similarity of \textbf{0.25}.

The extracted semantic descriptions also assigned more categories per building than the manual evaluation. Manual interpretation assigned an average of \textbf{1.39} categories per building, whereas the free-text extraction assigned \textbf{2.38} categories on average.

The strongest performance was observed for the category \textit{SHIFTED}, which reached a precision of \textbf{0.80}, a recall of \textbf{0.51}, and an F1-score of \textbf{0.62}. Moderate performance was observed for \textit{SHAPE\_MISMATCH}, with precision and recall values of approximately \textbf{0.42} and \textbf{0.43}, respectively.

Strong overprediction behaviour was visible for \textit{EXTRA\_PARTS} and \textit{MISSING\_PARTS}. The extraction procedure assigned \textit{EXTRA\_PARTS} 956 times although manual interpretation identified only 31 occurrences. Similarly, \textit{MISSING\_PARTS} was extracted 898 times compared to 95 manually assigned cases.

The weakest performance was observed for \textit{OVERSIMPLIFIED}. Only one correctly identified occurrence was found across the complete evaluation dataset.

In addition to the rule-based keyword analysis, a smaller qualitative evaluation was performed for the generated free-text descriptions. In this step, 100 generated sentences were manually reviewed in a separate evaluation interface. The evaluation was stopped after this subset because the generated descriptions showed strong repetition. Many sentences followed nearly the same structure and repeatedly described the polygon as having \textit{missing parts}, \textit{extra parts}, or both, even when the specific visual error differed.

After keeping the latest evaluation for each building, 99 unique sentence evaluations remained. Of these, only 11 sentences were classified as \textit{correct}. A further 25 were classified as \textit{partly correct}. The largest group was \textit{too vague}, with 37 cases, while 27 sentences were classified as \textit{wrong}. This means that only about one third of the reviewed descriptions were at least partially useful, whereas the majority were either too generic or incorrect.
